You have been training for several months but your progress has stalled? You are going from session to session without a real long-term plan? Chances are your program is missing **periodization**. This concept, used by every high-level athlete, is the key to lasting progress.

## What is periodization?

Periodization is the practice of organizing your training into **structured cycles** that alternate phases of intensity, volume, and recovery. Rather than doing the same routine indefinitely, you plan periods with specific objectives.

The fundamental idea is simple: the body adapts to the stress imposed on it. If the stimulus stays the same, progress stops. Periodization allows you to vary that stimulus in a smart way.

## The different levels of planning

A periodized program is organized on three levels:

- **Macrocycle** (3 to 12 months): The overall goal. For example, gaining 5 kg of muscle in 6 months or preparing for a competition.
- **Mesocycle** (3 to 6 weeks): A training block with a specific focus. For example, a strength mesocycle, a hypertrophy mesocycle, or a deload mesocycle.
- **Microcycle** (1 week): The concrete organization of your training week, session by session.

## Periodization models

**Linear periodization:** You progressively increase intensity (loads) while reducing volume (reps). This is the simplest model, ideal for beginners. For example:

- Weeks 1-4: 4 sets of 12 reps at 65% of 1RM
- Weeks 5-8: 4 sets of 8 reps at 75% of 1RM
- Weeks 9-12: 5 sets of 5 reps at 85% of 1RM

**Undulating periodization:** You alternate stimulus types within the same week. For example, Monday for strength (5x5 heavy), Wednesday for hypertrophy (4x10 moderate), Friday for muscular endurance (3x15 light). This model works well for intermediate and advanced trainees.

**Block periodization:** Each mesocycle focuses on a single physical quality. An accumulation block (high volume), then an intensification block (heavy loads), then a realization block (peak performance). Used by strength athletes.

## The importance of deloading

A **deload** is a programmed recovery week, generally every 4 to 6 weeks. During this week, you reduce volume and/or intensity by 40 to 60%.

Many trainees see the deload as a waste of time. It is quite the opposite: it is during recovery that muscles rebuild and grow stronger. Without deloading, you accumulate fatigue that eventually slows your progress or even causes injuries.

## How to know when to change cycles?

Several signals indicate it is time to move to the next cycle:

- **Stalling weights** for 2-3 consecutive weeks
- **Chronically high RPE** (9-10 on every set)
- **Persistent fatigue** despite proper sleep
- **Loss of motivation** or boredom with exercises

This is where precise tracking of your performance becomes crucial. Without data, you are navigating blind.
