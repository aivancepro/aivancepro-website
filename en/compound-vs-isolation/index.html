Compound vs Isolation: Which Type of Exercise Should You Choose?

Multi-joint or single-joint? Understand the difference and learn how to combine them for an optimal program.

Compound exercises (multi-joint)

Compound exercises engage multiple joints and muscle groups simultaneously. They allow you to lift heavier loads and recruit maximum muscle fibers in a single movement.

They are the foundation of any good training program. They develop functional strength and burn more calories per rep.

Compound exercise examples

Squat

Quads, glutes, hamstrings, core

Bench Press

Chest, triceps, anterior shoulders

Deadlift

Back, glutes, hamstrings, traps

Pull-ups

Lats, biceps, rhomboids

Overhead Press

Deltoids, triceps, traps

Barbell Row

Back, biceps, traps, rhomboids

Isolation exercises (single-joint)

Isolation exercises target a single muscle or muscle group by engaging only one joint. They are perfect for correcting imbalances, targeting a lagging muscle or finishing a workout with specific volume.

Isolation exercise examples

Bicep Curl

Biceps only

Tricep Extensions

Triceps only

Lateral Raises

Lateral deltoid

Leg Curl

Hamstrings

Leg Extension

Quadriceps

Dumbbell Flyes

When to use each type?

Start with compounds. Always place your multi-joint exercises at the beginning of your session when you're fresh and can lift heavy safely.

Finish with isolation. Use isolation exercises at the end of your session to target muscles that weren't sufficiently stimulated, or to add volume on a weak point.

The 80/20 rule

A good program generally follows the 80/20 rule: 80% compound exercises, 20% isolation. This ratio ensures a solid strength foundation while allowing targeted work.
