Nutrition and weight training: the basics for progress

March 3, 2026 · 7 min read

It is often said that fitness is 70% diet. That may be an exaggeration, but one thing is certain: without proper nutrition, even the best training program will only deliver mediocre results. Here are the fundamentals of nutrition for weight training.

Calories: the foundation of everything

Before discussing macros and timing, you need to understand the concept of caloric balance:

  • Caloric surplus = you eat more than your needs → weight gain (muscle + some fat)
  • Caloric deficit = you eat less than your needs → weight loss (fat + potentially some muscle)
  • Maintenance = you eat at your needs → body recomposition possible (especially for beginners)

To estimate your needs, calculate your basal metabolic rate (Mifflin-St Jeor formula) then multiply by your activity level. For bulking, add 200 to 400 kcal. For cutting, subtract 300 to 500 kcal.

Protein: the building material

Proteins are made up of amino acids, the building blocks of muscle. Without sufficient protein intake, muscle protein synthesis cannot occur properly.

Recommended intake: 1.6 to 2.2 g per kg of body weight. An 80 kg trainee should aim for between 128 and 176 g of protein per day.

Best sources:

  • Lean meat: chicken, turkey, lean beef (20-25 g protein per 100 g)
  • Fish: salmon, tuna, cod (also rich in omega-3)
  • Eggs: complete and affordable source (6 g per egg)
  • Legumes: lentils, chickpeas (great plant-based option)
  • Dairy: cottage cheese, skyr, Greek yogurt
  • Whey protein: convenient as a supplement, not mandatory

Carbohydrates: the fuel for training

Carbohydrates are the primary fuel during weight training sessions. Muscle glycogen reserves power your intense sets. Without enough carbs, your performance drops.

Recommended intake: 3 to 6 g per kg of body weight depending on your training volume.

Favor low to moderate glycemic index sources for steady energy:

  • Brown rice, sweet potato, oats: excellent sources of complex carbs
  • Fruits: banana (perfect around training), apple, berries
  • Whole grain bread, whole grain pasta: practical everyday options

Fats: do not skip them

Fats play a crucial role in hormone production, particularly testosterone. A fat intake that is too low (below 0.7 g/kg) can harm your hormone levels and therefore your progress.

Recommended intake: 0.8 to 1.2 g per kg of body weight.

Favor:

  • Olive oil, avocado: monounsaturated fatty acids
  • Nuts, almonds: convenient and calorie-dense snack
  • Fatty fish: anti-inflammatory omega-3s
  • Egg yolks: cholesterol needed for hormone production

Meal timing

The question of timing is often overrated. The essential thing is to hit your daily calorie and macro goals. However, a few simple principles can optimize your results:

  • Spread your protein across 3 to 5 meals (20-40 g per serving) to maximize muscle protein synthesis
  • Eat carbs before and after training for performance and recovery
  • Do not train fasted if your goal is muscle gain

Hydration

Often overlooked, hydration is essential. Even a 2% dehydration can reduce your performance by 10 to 20%. Aim for at least 2 liters of water per day, plus an additional 500 ml to 1 liter on training days.

Sample breakdown for an 80 kg male bulking

  • Calories: ~2800 kcal/day
  • Protein: 160 g (640 kcal)
  • Fat: 80 g (720 kcal)
  • Carbs: 360 g (1440 kcal)
